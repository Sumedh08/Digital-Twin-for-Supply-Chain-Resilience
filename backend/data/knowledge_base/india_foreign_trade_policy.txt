=== INDIA FOREIGN TRADE POLICY 2023 — STEEL AND MINERALS FOCUS ===
Source: DGFT Notification No. 01/2023 dated 31 March 2023

CHAPTER 1: OBJECTIVES AND STRATEGY
1.01: The FTP aims to make India a significant participant in world trade by 2030 through export diversification, market access improvement, and trade facilitation.
1.02: Special focus on value-added manufacturing exports including steel, engineering goods, chemicals and pharmaceuticals.

CHAPTER 2: STATUS HOLDER AND INCENTIVE FRAMEWORK
2.01: Export houses are classified as One Star, Two Star, Three Star, Four Star, and Five Star Export Houses based on export performance.
2.02: Benefits include priority processing of licences, self-certification for origin, and marketing support.

CHAPTER 3: STEEL SECTOR SPECIFIC PROVISIONS

3.1 — Advance Authorisation for Steel Manufacturers
Steel manufacturers may import iron ore, coking coal, and ferroalloys duty-free under Advance Authorisation for producing HRC, CRC, and coated steel for export. Standard Input-Output Norms (SION) apply:
- SION A-3003: Iron Ore Fines to Hot Rolled Coil — 1.62 tonnes input per tonne output.
- SION A-3005: Coking Coal to Steel products — varies by process route (BF-BOF, EAF-IF).

3.2 — Minimum Export Price (MEP)
The Government may impose MEP on steel products when domestic availability is constrained. As of 2023, no MEP is active on any steel product category.

3.3 — Steel Import Monitoring System (SIMS)
Registration under SIMS is mandatory for import of steel products. While this is an import provision, it affects exporters by ensuring domestic price stability which impacts export competitiveness.

CHAPTER 4: SPECIAL ECONOMIC ZONES (SEZ)
4.01: Steel mills in SEZs enjoy: duty-free import of raw materials, exemption from GST on inter-unit transfers, simplified customs procedures.
4.02: Net Foreign Exchange Earning (NFEE) requirement: SEZ units must achieve positive NFEE over a 5-year block period.

CHAPTER 5: TRADE REMEDIES AND ANTI-DUMPING
5.01: India's Directorate General of Trade Remedies (DGTR) investigates anti-dumping petitions from domestic industry.
5.02: Export subsidies that could trigger countervailing duties in destination countries should be avoided. CBAM compliance falls under this consideration as EU may treat certain Indian subsidies as shadow carbon subsidies.

CHAPTER 6: RULES OF ORIGIN
6.01: India is party to multiple FTAs (ASEAN, Japan, South Korea, Australia, UAE). Each FTA has specific Rules of Origin for steel products.
6.02: Certificate of Origin must be issued by authorised agencies (Export Inspection Council, FIEO, or designated Chambers of Commerce).
6.03: Value Addition Criteria for steel under most FTAs: minimum 35-40% domestic value addition to qualify for preferential tariff.

CHAPTER 7: LOGISTICS AND PORT PROVISIONS
7.01: Major steel export ports: Paradip (Odisha), Visakhapatnam (Andhra Pradesh), Haldia (West Bengal), Mundra (Gujarat).
7.02: Authorized Economic Operator (AEO) status available for steel exporters meeting compliance criteria. Benefits include deferred duty payment, reduced inspection, and priority processing.
