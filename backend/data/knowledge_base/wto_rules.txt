=== WORLD TRADE ORGANIZATION (WTO) RULES ===
Source: General Agreement on Tariffs and Trade (GATT 1994), TBT Agreement, SPS Agreement, Dispute Settlement Understanding

PART I: GATT 1994 — CORE ARTICLES RELEVANT TO CBAM AND TRADE

ARTICLE I — MOST-FAVOURED-NATION TREATMENT (MFN)
1. With respect to customs duties and charges of any kind imposed on or in connection with importation or exportation, any advantage, favour, privilege or immunity granted by any contracting party to any product originating in or destined for any other country shall be accorded immediately and unconditionally to the like product originating in or destined for the territories of all other contracting parties.
CBAM Context: If CBAM treats steel from India differently than steel from Norway (exempt), this could violate MFN unless justified under Article XX.

ARTICLE III — NATIONAL TREATMENT
1. Internal taxes and other internal charges, laws, regulations and requirements affecting the internal sale, offering for sale, purchase, transport, distribution or use of products shall not be applied to imported or domestic products so as to afford protection to domestic production.
2. The products of the territory of any contracting party imported into the territory of any other contracting party shall not be subject, directly or indirectly, to internal taxes or other internal charges of any kind in excess of those applied to like domestic products.
CBAM Context: If CBAM imposes a carbon cost on imports that exceeds what domestic producers pay under EU ETS (considering free allowances), it may violate National Treatment.

ARTICLE XI — GENERAL ELIMINATION OF QUANTITATIVE RESTRICTIONS
No prohibitions or restrictions other than duties, taxes or other charges, whether made effective through quotas, import or export licences or other measures, shall be instituted or maintained by any contracting party on the importation of any product of the territory of any other contracting party.

ARTICLE XX — GENERAL EXCEPTIONS
Subject to the requirement that such measures are not applied in a manner which would constitute a means of arbitrary or unjustifiable discrimination between countries where the same conditions prevail, or a disguised restriction on international trade, nothing in this Agreement shall be construed to prevent the adoption or enforcement by any contracting party of measures:
(a) necessary to protect public morals;
(b) necessary to protect human, animal or plant life or health;
(d) necessary to secure compliance with laws or regulations which are not inconsistent with the provisions of this Agreement;
(g) relating to the conservation of exhaustible natural resources if such measures are made effective in conjunction with restrictions on domestic production or consumption;
(j) essential to the acquisition or distribution of products in general or local short supply.

CBAM and Article XX Analysis:
- The EU claims CBAM is justified under Article XX(b) (environmental protection) and XX(g) (conservation of exhaustible natural resources — the atmosphere).
- The "chapeau" requires that CBAM is not "arbitrary or unjustifiable discrimination." Developing countries argue CBAM fails this test because: (1) it ignores common-but-differentiated-responsibilities (CBDR) under the Paris Agreement; (2) it disproportionately burdens countries with less green technology.
- WTO Appellate Body precedent (US-Shrimp, Brazil-Retreaded Tyres) suggests environmental measures CAN be justified, but must demonstrate nexus between the measure and the environmental objective.

PART II: TECHNICAL BARRIERS TO TRADE (TBT) AGREEMENT

ARTICLE 2.1: Members shall ensure that technical regulations are not prepared, adopted or applied with a view to or with the effect of creating unnecessary obstacles to international trade.
ARTICLE 2.2: Technical regulations shall not be more trade-restrictive than necessary to fulfil a legitimate objective (including environmental protection).
CBAM Context: Carbon reporting methodologies required by CBAM could be challenged as technical barriers if they impose disproportionate compliance costs on exporters from developing countries.

PART III: DISPUTE SETTLEMENT UNDERSTANDING (DSU)

Key provisions:
- ARTICLE 3: Disputes are resolved through consultation, panel formation, and appeal.
- ARTICLE 21: Reasonable period for compliance is typically 15 months.
- ARTICLE 22: If compliance fails, the complaining party may request compensation or suspend concessions (retaliation).

Current CBAM-Related Trade Tensions:
- India, China, South Africa, and other developing countries have expressed concerns at WTO about CBAM.
- India's position: CBAM violates CBDR principle and imposes unilateral carbon pricing on developing countries.
- No formal WTO dispute has been filed against CBAM as of early 2024, but several countries are considering it.
- The EU maintains CBAM is WTO-compatible as it mirrors the carbon cost borne by domestic EU producers.

PART IV: SUBSIDIES AND COUNTERVAILING MEASURES (SCM) AGREEMENT

ARTICLE 1 — DEFINITION OF A SUBSIDY
A subsidy exists when there is a financial contribution by a government or public body, and a benefit is conferred.
ARTICLE 3 — PROHIBITED SUBSIDIES
Export subsidies and subsidies contingent on the use of domestic over imported goods are prohibited.
CBAM Context: Some argue that free EU ETS allowances to domestic producers constitute a subsidy. If so, then charging importers a CBAM certificate while giving domestic producers free allowances could be discriminatory.
